AquaSonic

Acoustic Manipulation of Underwater Data Center Operations and Resource Management

Underwater data centers (UDCs) hold promise as next-generation data storage due to their energy efficiency and environmental sustainability benefits. While the natural cooling properties of water save power, the isolated aquatic environment and long-range sound propagation characteristics in water create unique vulnerabilities which differ from those of on-land data centers.

Our research discovers the unique vulnerabilities of fault-tolerant storage devices, resource allocation software, and distributed file systems to acoustic injection attacks in UDCs.

We investigate and discuss the ineffectiveness of standard defenses against acoustic injection attacks and formulate a novel machine learning-based detection system that reaches 96% True Positive and 1.75% False Negative. With this work, we aim to help manufacturers proactively protect UDCs against acoustic injection attacks and ensure the security of subsea computing infrastructures.

To appear in IEEE S&P 2024

Q&A

  • What are underwater data centers?


    Underwater data centers are metal enclosures containing all of the components of traditional data centers (servers, cables, routers, etc.). These data centers are filled with nitrogen gas to prevent corrosion of the components inside. They are also intended to last for years at a time without intervention because it is difficult to send humans underwater to maintain the server hardware. Microsoft, Subsea Cloud, and Offshore Oil Engineering Company, among others, have deployed successful prototypes and released underwater data centers (UDCs) to the market.


  • Are underwater data centers practical?


    Yes! Underwater data centers are practical because the surrounding water cools the many servers in the data center in a more efficient, environmentally sustainable way than the cooling systems of traditional on-land data centers. As the sizes of data centers and their storage requirements increase with the recent spike in demand for AI and cloud computing, it will become more important to cool them efficiently.


  • How does the AquaSonic attack work?


    We discover that underwater propagation of sound at certain frequency ranges can severely degrade the performance and capabilities of cloud servers enclosed in submerged metallic structures.

    This can be used by malicious adversaries to:

    • Slow down or completely block access to distributed databases and other data-heavy. applications running on cloud servers.
    • Manipulate how resources are distributed in cloud services.
    • Cause errors in data stored in such servers and failures in fault tolerance systems (e.g., RAID).


  • Why does it matter if an adversary can block access to data centers and data applications on cloud servers?


    Information relating to time-sensitive, safety-critical systems for healthcare, government, and military operations is stored in data centers. Disrupting access to this critical information can have catastrophic consequences.


  • How does this work?


    Data centers store data in servers which contain hard disk drives. Hard drives are made of movable mechanical components that are sensitive to vibrations. Sound waves propagate through water and convert to mechanical energy when they travel through solids. This mechanical energy reaches the drives in the enclosure and vibrates their internal components degrading the capability to read and write information from them. We found that by controlling the intensity (volume) of the sound, an attacker can control such degradation to manipulate cloud applications and resource management systems.


  • Who can mount the attack?


    This attack can be mounted by an adversary who has access to a speaker which can produce sufficiently high volume sounds (we used a commercially available pool and diving speaker) in the frequency range required to induce sufficient vibrations. The attacker must also be able to aim their speaker towards the target data center for the duration of the attack.


  • I have heard of acoustic attacks on HDDs before. How is this different?


    Previous research has demonstrated that loud sounds in air can cause laptop operating systems to crash. In this work, we investigate how a similar attack can potentially impact data center infrastructure on a broader scale and how the underwater environment opens data centers up to greater risk. The attack is particularly potent in water because sound travels 4 times faster than air and attenuates more slowly. Beyond sound properties, the isolated subsea environment of underwater data centers means that human operators cannot be easily dispatched if HDDs are damaged.


  • Does the attack matter with redundant storage systems like RAID or distributed databases?


    The attack matters with redundant storage systems like RAID or distributed databases because the redundancy in these systems is designed to protect data in case of disk failure, but the attack can induce failure in multiple disks simultaneously. This highlights the need for data protection systems that do not rely on the assumption that disks will fail independently.


  • Are Solid-state hard drives (SSDs) affected by this attack?


    No, since they are not composed of moving mechanical components which can vibrate. However, modern data centers rely on hybrid architectures composed of SSDs and HDDs for many reasons, including the need for large storage capacity, high reliability, and lower costs. Our experiments show that the attack increases the maximum delay time in hybrid architectures by 300%.


  • Why does the sound frequency matter?


    All solid objects have resonant frequencies, which are the frequencies at which the object naturally vibrates. The resonant frequencies of objects depend on what they are made of, their shape, and structure. Only sounds emitted at those frequencies can cause the resonance effect.


  • What is the range of the AquaSonic attack?


    We are able to induce up to 60% reduction in performance more than 6 meters away from the enclosure in our outdoor experiments using a commercial speaker. We can theoretically induce a 131.2 nm displacement in an actual-size data center enclosure from 1 kilometer away.


  • Does the AquaSonic attack require special equipment?


    No, this attack requires an underwater speaker with sufficient volume which can produce sounds at the resonant frequency of the target structure. We used a commercially available speaker used to communicate with divers and swimmers.


  • How can we protect against the AquaSonic attack?


    There are a variety of ways to defend against the AquaSonic attack. Some of these include designing the data center structure in a way to minimizes vibration propagation, adding materials around the servers to attenuate sound and vibrations, active noise cancellation, modifying the firmware of the HDDs, adding physical security around the data center, and using ML-based methods to detect changes in disk behavior to indicate whether an attack is occurring.


  • Has this attack been performed in the wild?


    No, we have not yet seen any indication that this attack has been performed in the wild.


  • Is the sound used in the attack safe?


    We use a commercially available speaker which can reach a maximum volume of 180 dB SPL, which is far lower than the capability of sonar, which can reach a volume of 220 dB SPL (remember that in the decibel scale, every 10 dB increase is approximately 10 times louder, so 220 dB SPL is approximately 10000 times louder than the 180 dB SPL sound). We also use hearing protection when performing our experiments.
