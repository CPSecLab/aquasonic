AquaSonic

Acoustic Manipulation of Underwater Data Center Operations and Resource Management

Underwater data centers (UDCs) hold promise as next-generation data storage due to their energy efficiency and environmental sustainability benefits. While the natural cooling properties of water save power, the isolated aquatic environment and long-range sound propagation characteristics in water create unique vulnerabilities which differ from those of on-land data centers.

Our research discovers the unique vulnerabilities of fault-tolerant storage devices, resource allocation software, and distributed file systems to acoustic injection attacks in UDCs.

We investigate and discuss the ineffectiveness of standard defenses against acoustic injection attacks and formulate a novel machine learning-based detection system that reaches 96% True Positive and 1.75% False Negative. With this work, we aim to help manufacturers proactively protect UDCs against acoustic injection attacks and ensure the security of subsea computing infrastructures.

To appear in IEEE S&P 2024

Q&A

  • How does the AquaSonic attack work?


    By playing sounds of certain frequencies in the direction of data centers, attackers can control the performance of storage devices.

    Acoustic injection at the resonant frequency of an HDD can cause:

    • Slow down or completely block access to databases and other data-heavy applications
    • Control which resources applications run on for eavesdropping purposes
    • Destroy data stored in data centers


  • Why are data centers underwater?


    With the increasing interest in environmental sustainability and the surging data center market due to a recent spike in demand for AI and cloud computing, companies are actively seeking alternative methods to improve energy efficiency and to reduce operating costs. To this end, Microsoft, Subsea Cloud, and Offshore Oil Engineering Company, among others, have already deployed successful prototypes and released in the market underwater data centers (UDCs). Typical UDCs have submerged structures with metal pressurized vessels containing server racks and filled with nitrogen gas to prevent corrosion, which have demonstrated significant advantages due to the natural cooling properties of water, space efficiency, and renewable energy integration.


  • How does this work?


    Data centers store data with hard drives, and hard drives contain mechanical components that are sensitive to vibrations. Sound is a form of energy, and energy can be converted to different forms. In this case, sound waves propagate through the water and convert to mechanical energy when they reach the enclosure around the hard disk drives. This mechanical energy travels through the enclosure into the drives and vibrates their internal components.


  • So why isn’t this a problem in data centers on land?


    It is! Storage devices in standard land data centers have been known to have reduced performance during fire alarms and when workers yell at the storage devices. The attack is particularly potent in water because sound travels faster, so it can reach farther.


  • Why does the frequency matter?


    All objects have resonant frequencies, which are the frequencies at which the object vibrates the most severely. The resonant frequencies of objects depend on what they are made of and their shape.


  • What is the range of the AquaSonic attack?


    We are able to induce up to 60% reduction in performance at a distance above 6 meters away from a test enclosure and can theoretically reduce performance at up to 1 kilometer away from an actual-size data center enclosure.


  • Which devices are susceptible to the AquaSonic attack?


    Hard disk drives are susceptible to the attack.


  • Does the AquaSonic attack require special equipment?


    This attack requires an underwater speaker with sufficient volume which can produce sounds at the resonant frequency of the target structure. We used a commercially available speaker used to communicate with divers and swimmers.


  • How can we protect against the AquaSonic attack?


    There are a variety of ways to defend against the AquaSonic attack. Some of these include designing the data center in a way to minimize vibration propagation, switching to different storage devices, detecting the attack, and rerouting the workload to other devices.


  • Has this attack been performed in the wild?


    No, we have not yet seen any indication that this attack has been performed in the wild.
